I  ·  The Sketch

A Dream Drawn
in Pencil

In January 1957, a Danish architect named Jørn Utzon submitted entry number 218 to an international competition for a new national opera house in Sydney. His drawings were barely finished — gestural, lyrical, more dream than blueprint.

Eero Saarinen arrived late to the jury deliberations. He retrieved entry 218 from the rejected pile and declared it a work of genius. The shells, he said, would become one of the greatest buildings of the twentieth century.

III  ·  The Shell Problem

Every shell,
one sphere

Scroll to witness the breakthrough

The Problem · 1956–1961
Four years of failed attempts. Engineers couldn't find a mathematical formula to build the organic shell shapes Utzon had sketched.
The Solution · 1961
Utzon discovered that every shell could be cut from the surface of a single sphere — radius 75.2 m. Suddenly 2,194 unique shapes became one.
The Geometry · Spherical Sections
Six great shells, two groups of three, all sliced from the same sphere. The identical geometry made mass-prefabrication possible for the first time.

Opened by Queen Elizabeth II on 20 October 1973. Beethoven's Ninth Symphony was performed. Budget overrun: 1,457%. Jorn Utzon received the Pritzker Prize in 2003. He died in 2008, never having seen his building completed.
The Structure
1959–1962
The Foundation Rises

Construction began on the massive concrete podium. This stage involved projecting the platform out into Sydney Harbour on approximately 588 concrete piers, providing the base for the massive shells to come.

1963
Section Through the Halls

The longitudinal section revealed the complexity beneath the sails: two major performance halls and a stage tower rising through the roof, all supported by a labyrinth of over 1,000 rooms and functional spaces.

The Interior
1963–1965
A City Within a Building

Below the shells, the design accommodated a vast program: 2 major halls, 5 rehearsal studios, and hundreds of dressing rooms and offices, creating a self-contained cultural city.

1965
The Ribs of the Shells

The roof was constructed from 2,400 precast concrete segments forming 280 ribs. These were all derived from a single sphere with a radius of approximately 75 metres. The highest shell reaches 67 metres above sea level.

The Transition
1966–1967
The Departure

Utzon resigned in 1966 due to political pressure and funding disputes, leaving Australia never to return. While the exterior geometry was precisely defined, a new team of architects was appointed to complete the interiors.

1967–1972
The Shells Complete

By 1967, the last precast rib was placed, and the “impossible” silhouette finally stood whole above Sydney Harbour. The following years were dedicated to the complex installation of the glass walls and interior fit-outs.

V  ·  The Build

$102 Million
Over Budget

The original estimate was £3.5 million, to be completed in 1963. The final cost was A$102 million, delivered a decade late. The project nearly destroyed the careers of everyone involved — and produced one of humanity’s greatest buildings.

Jørn Utzon resigned in 1966 after a bitter dispute with the new government over fees and design control. He never returned to Australia. He never saw his building completed. In 2003, he received the Pritzker Prize — architecture’s highest honour.

VI  ·  The Skin

Self-Cleaning,
Forever White

The roof tiles were designed to clean themselves in Sydney’s rain. Glazed in two finishes — matte buff and gloss white — they create the shimmering chevron pattern that defines the building’s silhouette from every angle.

Manufactured by Höganäs of Sweden, each tile was inspected individually before installation. Arranged so the chevron pattern appears continuous from any viewing angle — a mathematical tour de force in itself.
